2016 Design Trends:
The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly

Great news, it’s that time of year again! The time of year when you’re bombarded with songs about a white Christmas despite the utter lack of snow, which, instead of jolting holiday cheer down your spine, acts as a constant reminder of the planet’s impending doom that is Global Warming. The time of year where you make a new years resolution only to break it four hours later (yes, I had a roommate whose resolution only lasted four hours). The time of year when you can listen to songs about Grandma being run over by a reindeer and still feel jolly (sorry Grandma’s one good hip). Yes, it is that time of year.

It’s also the magical time of year when all bloggers and voices of the interwebs decide to bless you fashionistas, techies, Perez Hilton Jrs., fitness fanatics, and whatever else you are, with countdowns to summarize the year you just lived through. This year, I’ve decided to do the same, except instead of a “Top 10 Super Duper Awesome and Incredible Design Trends of 2020teen”, I’ve created a randomly numbered list or two to summarize my thoughts on 2016’s #designlyfe. No countdowns, no order, just some links links and a few words. This is 2016 Design Trends: The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly (well, maybe not the ugly).

The Good

  1. Bold, Playful Typography

    Who said text couldn’t be art? Possibly a result of the flat design trend and the increasing accessibility of type production tools, 2016 felt like a big year for bold, playful type that focused on design just as much as it did function.

    It's true, I could've found a better image for this one, but it's a good intro to the next two trends. And I really liked it.

  2. Neo-Memphis Design

    Neo Memphis Design was created by a handful of whimsical Italian furniture designers who sought inspiration from pre-existing design movements: art deco, pop art, and 1950s Kitsch. In 2016, we saw this design style move into the digital world as we were exposed to striking geometric figures and patterns, bold colour palettes, and a departure from constrained, grid based design. Thank you, neo-memphis design, for making my imagination come to life again.

    The world is your oyster, get out there and explore.

  3. Abstract Swiss

    In a lot of ways, this trend is so similar, yet also so different from the Neo-Memphis design trend. Abstract Swiss design features typography as the primary design element, often focusing on sans-serif typefaces. This trend also features grids and asymmetric layouts, while focusing on simplicity, legibility and objectivity.

    See what I'm talking about: http://www.werkstatt.fr/

  4. Animation on the web

The Less Good

  1. Parallax

    I like this one. I really do. In fact, that's why I made this list "The Less Good" instead of "The Bad", as the title of this post suggested. I’m just tired of feeling like my computer is preparing to venture into outpace whenever I visit your website. My computer has work to do, it can’t be preparing for liftoff forever.

  2. Stock Photography

    The arch nemesis of every designer. Stock photography is wonderful as it gives people and companies access to professional looking photography for a relatively affordable price, but there’s nothing more defeating than when you find yourself on another website using the same imagery as you. It’s an especially big problem since the web is currently so driven by imagery.

  3. Pre-Loading Animations

    Like many users of the web, I’m really not a fan of loading animations. Sure, they can be super cool (here’s an example of a super cool loading animation), but they often aren’t needed. I am yet to work at a company where developers don’t speak about page load times and ways to minimize them on a daily basis, so the thought of designing, developing, and implementing a page loader seems counterproductive. Besides, your users are likely visiting your site for content… they shouldn’t be sitting around waiting for it to load.
