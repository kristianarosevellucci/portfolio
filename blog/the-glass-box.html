The Glass Box:
Why Designers Should Travel

In Short:

1. Nature is the best colour schemer.

2. Inspiration comes from places you’ll least expect it.

3. You’re forced out of your comfort zone.

4. You’re forced to expand your mind.

5. Your experiences give you perspective.

6. As do the people you meet.

7. Those bed bugs and jet lag give you access to an over exhausted mind and a whole new realm of creativity.

8. You learn to deal with stress more productively.

9. You find yourself (sort of).

In Long:

Her name was Catherine. When she was 40 she walked across England raising money for cancer research. She did this the year I was born. I was now in my 20s. Her children were older than me. They were also no longer on speaking terms, she was legally deaf and forgot her hearing aids. She had an ex husband. She was good at math. She found out one year the she had Aspergers: her entire life made sense to her the moment she found that out. It now made sense to me now as well.

Truth be told, her name wasn’t Catherine. And in all honesty, I’m terrible with names. So, truthfully, I don’t know what her name was. But I met Catherine on a small group tour through the Scottish Highlands on a sunny Scottish Saturday (there’s some alliteration you never thought you’d hear).

I had chosen to start my adventure in the UK because a lot of the design I really love comes from there. I felt that through indulging myself in their culture, I’d be able to break down my own creative boundaries. I would find myself wandering through museum after museum, absorbing thought provoking work from some of my favourite artists and designers. I could admire Barbara Kruger’s strong juxtaposition of vintage imagery and political jargon, but when the time came to put the same commentary in my own work, my mind ran dry. I could not identify with Aaron Draplin’s use of the thick line, or Simon Birch’s ability to arouse a spectators senses as they immersed themselves in my work, but I found them thrilling nonetheless.

A few times, I’d burst out of a museum, mind running with inspiration. This actually happened on a random weekend trip to Montreal. And for all the other times, I’d exit each gallery with a couple of ideas for things I’d like to try on future projects. On my walks home, I would notice the colour schemes of the land surrounding me. Scotland was green, but a dull green (I was there in April). Some muddy blues and browns mixed in. The land was beautiful, but totally different from Iceland’s deep burgundy’s, royal blues, charcoal greys and yellows. Different than Hawaii’s rich greens and turquoise blues, or Porto’s rich yellows and muted browns.

I’d have coffee with a complete stranger: sharing my utterly (pun intended?) random love for cows. I’d answer questions on the streets of Portugal about the bracelet on my wrist made from rubber and metal: a purchase from some South African orphans, made from found objects, yet more durable than any of the other carefully constructed bracelets on my arm.

And in all my adventures, I would find my creative blocks start to lessen and my mind start to expand. These moments, stories, colours and sounds becoming carefully woven into the narrative that would later inspire my own creations.

—

Are you feeling stunted? Is the emotional box that’s holding you back glass? Can you see the world going on around you? Good. Don’t panic. Just slow down from your busy life, take a breather, and feel your frustrations. Take advantage of this box and it’s glass walls: hit your head against them. For every hour you do this, you will burn on hundred and fifty calories. Now doesn’t that feel better? Excellent. You have been productive after all.

Stop worrying about everything, stop feeling inadequate. You are not alone in your frustration. You are with every creative you’ve ever encountered and you are with yourself. You are with your imagination. And with your imagination anything is possible because it allows you to look at life through the wrong end of a kaleidoscope… Every. Single. Time.

If you remember to breath, everything else will come with it. And maybe, just maybe, if you are lucky, Catherine will chisel down the walls of the glass box in which you are enclosed, and she will whisper lightly into your box “It is me, Carrol. It wasn’t until my 50’s when I learned about Aspergers and my life started to make sense”.

Carol. Her name was Carol all along.
